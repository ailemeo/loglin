Welcome! This interactive visualization will help you understand the popular technique of log-linear modeling. For a quick intro to the simple defining formulas, consult the second half of this homework handout.

Try it out: The sliders below control the parameters ("weights") of a log-linear model. When you increase the circle weight, which filled shapes get bigger? Which ones get smaller?

One game is to try to match all 4 shapes to the gray outlines. You will need to use both sliders. A shape will turn gray if it matches well. It turns red if it is too small, blue if it is too big. Note: You may like to zoom in with your browser.

What the picture means: Your model is defining a probability for each shape. When the weights are 0, all 4 shapes have equal probability of 1/4. This is shown by their equal areas.

But the empirical probability of solid circles is 1/2, because 30 of the 60 shapes in your training data are solid circles. This "target" of 1/2 is shown by the larger area outlined in gray.

Since your model currently says that p(solid circle) = 1/4, it incorrectly expects only about 15 of the 60 training shapes to be solid circles. By increasing the circle weight, you can improve your model until it predicts the observed count of 30. The observed count of 30 and the current expected count of 15 are contrasted in the solid circle box.
