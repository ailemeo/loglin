Same model, new dataset. The model is wrong in this case: the new dataset did not actually come from this 6-feature log-linear model. Its 9 probabilities cannot be matched well by manipulating the 6 parameters of this model.

So, just do the best you can with this model by following the gradient hints. At the optimum, there will be no red or blue left on the sliders (e.g., the total number of circles will be correct). However, there will still be red and blue left on the shapes (e.g., the counts for individual kinds of circles will be rather wrong).

Automatic parameter tuning. As a convenience, you can click the "Step" button. This moves all of the sliders slightly in the direction of their hints. Note that the "Step" button controls the sliders more precisely than your mouse can. The "Step size" field (which you can change) controls how far they move. However, a slider with a longer hint bar will always move farther than one with a shorter hint bar.

As a further convenience, you can click the "Solve" button. This clicks the "Step" button over and over for you until the log-likelihood is maximized. You can watch the log-likelihood bar get longer and longer as the solver continues to follow the hints.

(This is the method of gradient ascent. Other automatic solution methods would also work, such as improving or optimizing one weight at a time. Both methods require looping through all the weights repeatedly until all the hints are 0.)

Dangers. If the step size is too big, the solver could jump back and forth over the optimal weights without ever landing on them. Taking too big a step can even make the log-likelihood worse. So our solver gradually reduces the step size as it goes. It jumps quickly toward the right neighborhood at first, then starts to fine-tune so it can zero in on the exact answer.

Occasionally the solver may stop too early. You can try reducing the stepsize and clicking "Solve" again.
